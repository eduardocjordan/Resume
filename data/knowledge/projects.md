# Projects

Metrics: 200M+ organic impressions, +2.3% brand SOM (share of market), President's Outliers Award.

Metrics: HRC Best Place to Work, James E. Burke Award, LATAM Forum recognition.

## 03 — Neutrogena Sun Care Launch (Johnson & Johnson)
Unlocked a four-year regulatory stall by mapping the SPF 55+ COFEPRIS (Mexican regulatory) pathway — SPF 55+ is classified as a drug in Mexico, requiring a specific regulatory route. Coordinated regional production in Brazil and launched the category from zero in Mexico.

Metrics: new category created from zero, +10% incremental B2B revenue, new market share.

## 04 — Brand Built from Zero (Grupo Mariposa)
Built a brand and innovation system from scratch. Filtered 165 ideas down to one market-ready product through rigorous consumer validation gates (concept testing, sensory panels, pricing ladders).

Metrics: +35% revenue growth, digital-first export brand, full P&L ownership.

## Notable stories behind the work

**The brief nobody asked for.** At PepsiCo, Eduardo noticed limited-edition launches underperformed because of sequencing. He mapped data from 6 launches and proposed a trade activation adjustment that became standard practice — nobody asked for it; that's when the most useful improvements tend to happen.

**The regulatory detour.** Neutrogena's Sun Care line had been stalled at the Mexican border for 4 years. Eduardo diagnosed that SPF 55+ is legally classified as a drug in Mexico, requiring a COFEPRIS regulatory pathway. He coordinated regional production in Brazil and launched the category within 2 years. His framing: "Strategy without diagnosis is just confidence."

**The 165 ideas.** At Grupo Mariposa, Eduardo built the system to decide what was worth building — filtering 165 ideas through rigorous validation (concept testing, sensory panels, pricing ladders). Only one made it to market. That discipline is how you avoid launching products nobody wants.

**The packaging line crossover.** Eduardo's move into marketing was engineered, not accidental. As a PMO, he was running automation on packaging lines; the innovation team saw how he handled ambiguity and ran meetings. His technical credentials got him in the room — his performance in the room got him the marketing role.
